Inbetweeners 2: Eight things British people can't help doing abroad

To celebrate the latest cinematic jolly for everyone's favourite sex-starved students, co-creator Damon Beesley explains why the Brits suck at foreign travel

Sweating

Britain once had a global reputation for excellence in many things: engineering, football, cross-dressing, empire building, genocide. One by one, we've lost these crowns to new upstarts on the block like Costa Rica, Thailand and Vladimir Putin. Sadly, there are few things that we Britons can say we truly excel at these days. Except we produce sweat like no other nation. Put us in a slightly-above-room-temperature environment and it literally pours out of us. Buckets of the stuff. The second we step off the plane and feel the heat rising off the tarmac, we become sentient water features, leaving saline puddles in our wake as we huff and puff to the nearest air-conditioned building. We rule at sweating.

Hunting down amusing signs in badly written English

God help you if you've attempted to pass useful information on to your English-speaking patrons by writing a sign and then committed a grammatical faux pas within it. Your crime will be instantly Instagrammed and compiled into a Reddit meme list before you can say "curse you Google translate". You well-meaning idiots.

Swilling booze

Watching Britons drink booze abroad can't be dissimilar to witnessing the last few hours before prohibition was introduced to Chicago in 1920. Or the fall of Rome. We drink alcohol like someone is going to take it away from us. Maybe it's ying to the yang of sweating (see above, Sweating); what came first, the perspiration or the problem drinking? When Iain Morris and I went on a research trip to Malia for the first Inbetweeners movie we'd forgotten about this universal law for Brits abroad. We arrived on the main strip of bars at sunset, comfortably the oldest and sweatiest lifeforms there. After a few hours of being eyed with suspicion by the hordes of young Brits we let it slip that we wrote The Inbetweeners. Tragic, I know, but after that we were at least allowed to get on with the drinking without making anyone else feel too uncomfortable.

Displays of breathtaking superiority

Most Brits heading out into the world will broadly assume anywhere that lies longitudinally south of Calais is part of the third world. Usually this attitude manifests itself in the question: "Is it safe to drink the tap water?" I know it's hard to believe but even in Spain, the 14th largest economy in the world, they have adequate water sanitation. I'm not vouching for Bulgaria, though.

Speaking the lingo

Over the years we've become slightly more adventurous with foreign languages. These days, rather than simply pointing at stuff and speaking loudly in English we've developed a new form of multilingualism, which involves still speaking in English but with a subtle hint of a local accent. Maybe with the odd word of French or Spanish thrown in. We call this new tongue Joey Bartonese.

Experimenting with food

August 1989. Playa de Las Americas, Tenerife. The date is seared in my brain as the day I ate my first fresh tomato. It was mistakenly placed in, or rather not removed by me, from a sandwich I'd ordered while drunk (see above, Swilling booze). I was 18 years old and outraged. It's this sense of culinary adventure that foreign travel inspires in us all.

Following the rules

Let's be honest, not just following the rules but constant upkeep of the rules. Who else will ensure that nobody flouts the conventions of queuing. Or sort out territorial transgressions in overhead baggage compartments? Without us there wouldn't be a global tourism industry, there'd be chaos. And the Germans would be up at dawn reserving the best seats to watch the madness from.
