Title :

Jonathan Mills goes out to his own tune: music at this year's Edinburgh international festival

A composition by the outgoing festival director will feature in one of this year’s final concerts

Contributor :

Tom Service

Public Date :

Content :

Jonathan Mills’s last season as director of the Edinburgh international festival, which opens on Friday, brings some surprises and some resonant coincidences in his final music programme. There are just two staged operas – Berlioz’s Trojans with the Mariinsky Opera and Valery Gergiev, Aldeburgh Music’s acclaimed production of Britten’s Owen Wingrave – both of which speak to this year’s festival’s themes of the profound effects of conflict on art, whether through the portrayal of epic warfare or the trauma of personal conscience. And there’s also a concert performance of Rossini’s William Tell with conductor Gianandrea Noseda and his company of the Teatro Regio Turin.

The Usher Hall concerts’ highlights include two-concert residencies from the Czech Philharmonic and Jiri Belohlavek, bringing a home-grown smorgasbord of their bread-and-butter repertoire, and the Concertgebouw Orchestra and Mariss Jansons in an only slightly more adventurous but nonetheless staple diet of Shostakovich, Ravel, Rihm, Brahms and Strauss. The opening concert on Friday is almost the pick of the lot, as Oliver Knussen – born in Glasgow – conducts the Royal Scottish National Orchestra in Schoenberg’s Five Orchestral Pieces, Scriabin’s Prometheus and Debussy’s Martyrdom of St Sebastian; but there’s also Phillippe Herreweghe in Bach’s B Minor Mass and Stravinsky’s Symphony of Psalms, as well as Andrew Davis leading the Melbourne Symphony Orchestra in Percy Grainger’s masterpiece of creative imagination, The Warriors.

Most intriguingly of all, and potentially bravely, Jonathan Mills is going out with a musical bang of his very own creation, with a performance of his oratorio Sandakan Threnody in the first half of the final concert. Talking to Kate Molleson, Mills described his decision to include his own composition in his final orchestral programme (in the second half, Ilan Volkov conducts the BBC Scottish Symphony Orchestra in Janacek’s Glagolitic Mass): “I questioned myself about whether or not I would include a piece of my own. But I’ve been asked by a lot of people in Edinburgh, OK, you’re a composer, so let’s hear your music!

“Sandakan Threnody is a lament, a putting to rest of the souls of people that I never knew. But my father was in that prison camp in north Borneo [during the second world war, in which 2,500 British and Australian prisoners of war died]. It’s not actually a critique of the Japanese, it’s much more personal. It’s a statement of solidarity, of sadness, but of optimism as well for those troops and their plight. It’s a threnody in the truest Greek sense of the word, a grieving that has to be a public act. You can’t shove things under the carpet. We see that with great relevance today – in Libya, or Syria; we need to bear witness, we need to allow the voices of those who havebeen silenced to be heard. So this was a moment of deep personal emotion for me, which I was offering to all of the people with whom my father had suffered, before he died: to show that there was somebody who was taking that circumstance forward, simply using my skills – whether they were good or not is irrelevant – at the disposal of this particular moment.”

Sandakan Threnody obviously fits Mills’s themes of artistic responses to war, and however modestly he puts it in terms of his “skills”, it’s a bold statement to make your (almost) last word as the world’s most famous arts festival’s director your own composition.

As well as the Usher Hall gigs, there’s another season of variously exquisite and imaginative chamber concerts at the Queen’s Hall running throughout the three weeks, but my pick of the whole music programme are the concerts at Greyfriars, starting with a truly stellar line-up in Messiaen’s Quartet for the End of Time on Monday 11th (piano: Steven Osborne, cello: Alban Gerhardt, violin: Antje Weithaas, clarinet: Jörg Widmann!), some magnificent vocal consorts, from the Hilliard Ensemble to the Polish Radio Choir and Sister Marie Keyrouz’s L’Ensemble de la Paix, and instrumental groups from the centuries-traversing programme of the Arditti Quartet, with countertenor Jake Arditti, to Concerto Italiano’s Monteverdi. If you’re lucky enough to be in Edinburgh over the next 22 days – enjoy!
