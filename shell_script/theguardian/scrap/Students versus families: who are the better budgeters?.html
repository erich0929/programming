Title :

Students versus families: who are the better budgeters?

Cash-strapped students or hard-working families - who's best at managing their money? We get to the bottom of it

Contributor :

Lawrence Wakefield

Public Date :

Friday 8 August 2014 09.53 BST

Content :

Good news, Save the Student have done their annual student spending survey and discovered that students are getting better at economising. They've toned down their average monthly spend to £735 (from £768 per month last year) – a 4.2% reduction.

Granted, it isn't a huge difference. But it the context of rising rent and travel costs, students have done well to bag a net saving by cutting back on clothes, bills and socialising.

The most impressive reduction is a £38 a month trim in food spending. Either students have finally given up on ready-meals and learned to cook, or there is a long running multi-buy on Super Noodles happening at Tesco.

So signs point to some impressively austere studying. But before we give the student population a pat on the back, we had better check how their spending compares to that of normal humans.

Are students better with their budgets than families? And when parents nag about money (or as they would put it - "give budgeting advice") is it based on any real wisdom? Or are they just talking a good game on the phone from Waitrose, while stocking up on pre-made dauphinoise potato?

There's only one way to find out – by cross-referencing the Save the Student figures with the Office of National Statistics data for average household spending (well, what else was I going to do with my Saturday night?).

By looking at five key areas of spending, we can see where the money is going, and crucially, who could be making more of a saving in each. Game on.

On-trend spends

Students are a fashion conscious bunch. Despite an £8 decrease in their monthly spend, 14.3% of the average student budget still goes on clothes. I blame the students at St Martins and Goldsmiths for pushing up the averages with their trendy clobber. The average household spends just 4.8% of it's total on clothes – including 40p a week on men's underwear.

Winner: average household

Home truths

The average household spends only 14% of it's monthly budget on rent and bills, yet due to the scandal that is student housing, university attendees are shelling out a whopping 57.5% of their budget on putting a roof over their heads. No wonder occupation protests are becoming so popular at universities - think of the savings.

Winner: average household

On yer bike

Students are triumphing over families in the travel stakes, spending just 6% of their money on getting around - 5% less than the average household. This could be down to students undying commitment to stay green by cycling and walking wherever possible. Or it might be that all the tories in Wiltshire running three Land Rovers are distorting the figures.

Winner: students

Credit crunching munching

Despite cutting their spending on food dramatically, students still spend a bigger portion of their reddies on food than Mr and Mrs Smith - 2.6% more, infact. I guess you can't put a price on good chili con carne.

Winner: average household

Crap you don't need

Perhaps the most surprising result of all. The "Other" spend in the surveys (a polite way of saying "consumerist confetti", or "crap you don't need") is actually lower for students than the average household. Despite all those hours spent browsing Amazon when they should be studying, it seems students are sticking to the essentials rather than splashing out on 3D tellies and antique vases.

Winner: students

FINAL VERDICT

Despite some clever cutbacks by student savers, they just can't compete with the economies of scale that come with family life. It's a victory for the (slightly less trendy) average households of Britain.
