Title :

Toothbrushing advice unacceptably inconsistent, study finds

Researchers looked at advice given by dental associations, toothbrush and toothpaste companies and in dental textbooks

Contributor :

Public Date :

Friday 8 August 2014 15.30 BST

Content :

Dental experts have found a worrying lack of agreement on how people should brush their teeth, a study by the British Dental Journal has found.

Researchers looked at brushing advice given by dental associations across 10 countries, toothbrush and toothpaste companies and in dental textbooks and found the results to be "unacceptably inconsistent".

The study found differing recommendations on what method to use when brushing, how often to brush and how long people should brush for.

Senior author Aubrey Sheiham, emeritus professor of dental public health at University College London's department of Epidemiology and Public Health, said: "The public needs to have sound information on the best method to brush their teeth.

"If people hear one thing from a dental association, another from a toothbrush company and something else from their dentist, no wonder they are confused about how to brush. In this study we found an unacceptably inconsistent array of advice from different sources."

Giving advice on teeth brushing, Prof Sheiham said: "Brush gently with a simple horizontal scrubbing motion, with the brush at a forty-five degree angle to get to the dental plaque.

"To avoid brushing too hard, hold the brush with a pencil grip rather than a fist. This simple method is perfectly effective at keeping your gums healthy.

"There is little point in brushing after eating sweets or sugary drinks to prevent tooth decay. It takes bacteria from food about two minutes to start producing acid, so if you brush your teeth a few minutes after eating sugary foods, the acid will have damaged the enamel."
