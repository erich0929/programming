Title :

Nicola Adams: 'It always felt like boxing was my path'

When Nicola Adams was growing up, women weren't allowed to be boxers. Now she's won Olympic and Commonwealth gold. Here she talks about making history against the odds – and why she still won't call herself a feminist

Contributor :

Homa Khaleeli

Public Date :

Friday 8 August 2014 15.04 BST

Content :

Sunglasses on and leaning moodily against a wall for the photographer, Nicola Adams looks every inch the sleek, stylish celebrity. At just 5ft 4in, and with a fighting weight of 51kg she's small, slight and carefully dressed. Then she bends down to unzip her white trainers for the camera and her face relaxes into a grin, "I'd better remember I've done this or I'll fall on my face," she laughs.

It's a typical Adams moment; puncturing any hint of self-importance. In 2012 she was the first female boxer to win a gold medal at the Olympic Games. Asked how she would celebrate her win, she told a stunned-looking commentator, "I think I just want to go to Nando's."

Two years later, she is the first female boxing champion at the Commonwealth games. "To create history once in your lifetime is amazing, but to do it twice is unbelievable," she tells me over a cup of coffee (a vanilla latte with extra cream – she is taking a break from her usual strict diet).

Despite Usain Bolt reportedly calling the Commonwealth Games a "bit shit", Adams insists that winning gold felt even better than it did at the Olympics. "I thought I would feel exactly the same, but actually it feels more … " she trails off trying to express the enormity of it. "It could still be sinking in, but to actually do it again feels surreal."

Going into an international competition as an established champion, rather than the scrappy outsider, piled on the expectations. "Winning Olympic gold put me in everyone's focus," she says. "It's hard when everyone thinks you will sail through, because you are the person who has to do it. It's a good thing I don't feel pressure in a big way because otherwise I would have crumbled."

One of the reason's Adams seems so impressive is that she always makes a tough fight look easy. In the ring, she makes sure she is happy, relaxed and smiling so her opponents never see her nervous, and they know that she has full confidence in a win. "Boxing is certainly not about going in, seeing a red mist and throwing punches," she explains. "If you do that, you will get caught. The first thing you learn is defence – and there is a lot of technique to master."

When I ask her about being a bisexual black woman in such a macho sport, she insists there have been no problems. Today she is happy to admit she is in a relationship ("I think it's nice to show different sides of me," she says), but agrees that when she first arrived in the public eye she was wary of talking about her sexuality.

"When I started doing interviews I never knew what to say, so I never mentioned it. But I started to feel like I was going into myself. I didn't want to hide anything – it's not something I would ever hide, and I don't want people to think I am ashamed of it."

Her family knew she was bisexual from the age of 16 or 17, and "it was never a big deal". Of course, she was nervous about telling her mother, she says, but "it was fine". Later I speak to Adams's mother on the phone, and she laughs about how drama-free the coming-out conversation was. "I kind of knew she was bisexual already, but I wasn't sure," she tells me. "I just said to her: 'I'm going to make a cup of tea now, do you want one?' It sounds terrible when she was telling me something so big, but what was there to say? We just carried on as normal."

Nicola Adams took to boxing aged 12 as an outlet during the difficult times she had following her parents' split. Photograph: Colorsport/Corbis

Adams was brought up on a council estate in Burmantofts Leeds, which she describes as "not the roughest, but rough enough" thanks to the local kids' fondness for nicking cars. She was always a tomboy, and adored watching videos of the old fights of Sugar Ray Leonard and Muhammad Ali, with her father, Innocent. But then, when she was 12 years old, her parents split up and her father and older half-brother moved out. "I missed them, and it was a difficult time. I'd see my friends, and their parents were still together while mine had split up. I was angry at times."

The local boxing gym became a refuge, she says. "I only went into a gym by accident. My mum couldn't get a babysitter and wanted to do aerobics, so she took me and Kurtis, my younger brother, down to the gym. There was an after-school boxing class on with some of the kids from school." By the end of that first session, Adams couldn't wait to go back. "There weren't any other girls there, but I didn't mind," she says, "I loved it." Her mother, she says, was happy to see her off the streets. "She wasn't worried," Adams laughs, "because the first time she saw me spar, I was beating up the boys."

The club gave her an escape from the upheaval at home and became, "almost like another family", one where her coaches became father figures for her. More importantly, Adams, still barely a teenager, felt she had discovered what she wanted to do with her life. "Even though women's boxing wasn't an Olympic sport then, I knew I would be an Olympic champion. Most people must've thought, 'It's never going to happen.' So I don't know why … it just felt like that was my path, and that one day I would achieve it."

What makes her ambition all the more extraordinary was the dire state of women's boxing at the time. Until 1996 it had been banned for a staggering 116 years by the Amateur Boxing Association of England. And when the first amateur bout was announced in 1997 – a year after Adams started her after-school class – the contest, between two 13-year-old girls, was dubbed a "freakshow" by high-profile boxing managers, and denounced as something that would only be watched by "yobbos" for titillation.

Looking back, it can be hard to remember the strength of the opposition. Even as late as 2008, Jane Couch, the then British welterweight champion of the world, had to go to a tribunal to overturn a ban on her fighting in the UK. The court was told the British Boxing Board of Control believed that PMT made women too unstable to fight.

When it was finally announced as an Olympic sport in 2009, former Olympic medallist Amir Khan declared that, "deep down I think women shouldn't fight," while the Amateur International Boxing Association suggested women boxers should wear skirts, with some coaches claiming this would make them seem more "elegant".

Adams acknowledges that women have "always had to fight" for equality, and makes a link between the struggle of women boxers, and the struggle for women to get the vote. Yet despite this, she says "I wouldn't call myself a feminist", and for a moment her smile falters.

"I am just me," she says, frustrated by the line of questioning. "I like boxing and acting and doing my charity work." She says she doesn't think that girls are put off sport because of the scrutiny around female appearance – "I wasn't" – and, perhaps because of her lower profile (or tougher image), says she has never faced the sort of criticism or insults that Rebecca Adlington has endured. Even if she did, she says, she would ignore them. "My family and friends are the only people I pay mind to," she says firmly. "It's none of my business what anyone else thinks. I don't even know them, so why should I care?"

There were plenty of challenges Adams says, but throughout her early career she remained adamant that she would succeed. Partly, this was down to the immense support from her family. "My mum did really well raising me and my brother by herself. I know it was a struggle, and even from a young age, when I was boxing, it was always to make my mother proud."

Their bond was strengthened when Adams was 13 and her mother fell dangerously ill with meningitis. Adams was left to care for her, and Kurtis, who was still in primary school. "We thought she was going to die," she remembers. "She was really sick, weak, and in a lot of pain. I was scared about how me and my brother would survive. Who would look after us? Would we move and change schools? I just knew I had to help and do the things she couldn't. So I got the shopping in, washed clothes, cooked, did my homework. I went to school as normal, picked my brother up afterwards and went to the hospital to see my mum. I just tried to reassure Mum that everything was fine."

Her mother made a full recovery, but as Adams' boxing skills improved, the costs of her training and equipment became almost crippling. Her mother, who worked as a restaurant manager until 11pm or midnight, found it hard to make ends meet, and although she worked tirelessly to keep her daughter in the minds of funding bodies and the media, no money was forthcoming. Instead Adams took on a series of jobs, from working on a building site to being an extra on Emmerdale and Coronation Street, and between them the family managed to cover the costs of sending her to international competitions. Today the pair are still very close, and Adams admits that her mother calls her a mummy's girl – "but she's the only one allowed to call me that!"

The family's toughest test came in 2009, when Adams fell down the stairs at her home while rushing to get to a fight on time. She carried on regardless, and won the match, but after days of increasing pain she discovered she had broken a bone in her back, which left her bed-ridden for three months. "It was the toughest thing I have faced. I felt like giving up. I didn't think I would be able to box again, although the doctors had told me I would. I just didn't see how I could get there.

When it came to the last trial for the Team GB squad, Adams was told she had to take part to have a chance, despite not having boxed for a year. "I was on morphine patches," she remembers. "I was supposed to just have one, but I stuck two on my arm, took some pills and thought: 'I am just going to do it.' Luckily they saw something there. But for the whole weekend after that I couldn't move at all – I was in so much pain."

Now, despite the physical demands of her career, she says she would consider starting a family herself. "Mary Kom [the Indian five times World Amateur Champion boxer] had twins and still won a medal," she points out. "She's just had another child and is still coming back into the sport."

But for the moment, she is concentrating on boxing. She has still only won a silver medal at the World Amateur Championships, and wants to rectify that. Then there is the small matter of the 2016 Rio Games, which she hopes will make her the first British boxer to be a double Olympic champion. Oh, and she wouldn't mind starring in an action movie like the Fast and the Furious franchise.

On top of all that, there is the temptation to turn professional, and take part in money-making fights, as Amir Khan has now done. That could be a tricky prospect because there are only a handful of professional female boxers in the UK and it might be difficult for her to find enough competitors of her calibre. "But I guess it just takes a few big names to change that," she muses. "Maybe I will be the first there as well," she laughs. And who would dare to argue?
