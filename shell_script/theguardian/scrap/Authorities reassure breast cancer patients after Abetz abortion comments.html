Title :

Authorities reassure breast cancer patients after Abetz abortion comments

Australian Greens and Labor call for Liberal Eric Abetz to apologise and cancel a function with anti-abortion campaigners

Contributor :

Daniel Hurst

Public Date :

Friday 8 August 2014 10.42 BST

Content :

More than 40 Australian women were diagnosed with breast cancer on the day a political storm developed over comments by a senior Abbott government minister referencing a discredited link to abortion.

The Breast Cancer Network Australia sought to reassure women that there was no evidence of a link between abortion and breast cancer, saying people experienced shock when diagnosed and the last thing they needed was “any further and unnecessary anxiety”.

The government’s leader in the Senate and employment minister, Eric Abetz, denied endorsing the claim but faced calls to apologise for referring to it in a television interview.

The acting leader of the Greens, Adam Bandt, said Abetz should say sorry for insulting “not just any woman who has suffered breast cancer or who has had an abortion, but all women”.

Labor sought to build pressure on Abetz to cancel plans to host lunch with a proponent of the view, the American anti-abortion campaigner and doctor Angela Lanfranchi, who also opposes use of the contraceptive pill.

The Australian Medical Association (AMA) was particularly scathing, saying the comments were “very unhelpful, both for people who might be struggling with the concept of termination of pregnancy … but also for people that have suffered or have family members that have suffered from breast cancer”.

Tony Abbott said he had spoken with Abetz, who was “feeling a little sheepish about the way his comments have skyrocketed” and would not repeat the remarks.

“That so-called research has long been discredited,” the prime minister said of the abortion-breast cancer claim.

In the interview with Ten’s The Project on Thursday night, Abetz said he was supporting an upcoming event of the World Congress of Families in Melbourne “based on their basic views and beliefs that they support the traditional view of marriage and that they are pro-life”.

When asked by the panellist and Mamamia publisher Mia Freedman whether he agreed with the “factually incorrect statement” promoted by Lanfranchi that abortion led to breast cancer, Abetz said: “I think the studies, and I think they date back from the 1950s, assert that there is a link between abortion and breast cancer.”

Freedman replied that it was “conclusively and scientifically incorrect” in the same way that the linking of vaccination to autism was incorrect.

Abetz: “Well, I don’t know what your scientific expertise is to be able to run that commentary; I confess that I don’t have the scientific expertise.”

Freedman: “It’s not me, it’s the AMA.”

Abetz: “Well there are other organisations that have differing views as some of these speakers are clinical professors.”

The alleged link has been rejected by leading health organisations including Cancer Australia, the World Health Organisation, the US National Cancer Institute, and the London-based Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists.

An American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists committee concluded in 2009: “Early studies of the relationship between prior induced abortion and breast cancer risk were methodologically flawed. More rigorous recent studies demonstrate no causal relationship between induced abortion and a subsequent increase in breast cancer risk.”

Abetz issued a statement saying it was incorrect for the media to claim he had drawn a link, or believed there was a link, between abortion and breast cancer.

“In my interview last night on The Project I studiously avoided doing so, and was cut off before being able to acknowledge that Dr Angela Lanfranchi’s views on this topic were not the accepted medical view,” he said.

Abetz said his support for the World Congress of Families did not mean he endorsed the view of every single speaker, although he defended Lanfranchi’s credentials as “a breast cancer surgeon and a clinical assistant professor of surgery”.

“I accept the AMA has a right to disagree with Dr Lanfranchi’s views,” Abetz said.

On 28 August Abetz is scheduled to host a luncheon at Parliament House for Lanfranchi and two other people listed to speak at the World Congress of Families in Melbourne: Louise Kirk and Dr Larry Jacobs.

Kirk is a UK-based campaigner who aims to “turn around the ghastliness of sex education” and is making a presentation in Melbourne titled “sexuality – a gift for life”. Jacobs is due to present at the same conference “on the pro-life and pro-family policies in the US and Russia”, according to the conference program.

While in Canberra, Lanfranchi is due to speak at an event organised by the Australian Capital Territory Right to Life Association.

Labor’s health spokeswoman, Catherine King, said Abetz’s interview comments were “appalling” and the statement issued on Friday morning fell short of the “complete retraction” required.

“I think the fact that he is hosting the person who is purporting this link at a luncheon at Parliament House says entirely that unfortunately he does endorse these comments and I think it goes to the heart of the prejudice frankly of this government,” King said.

“For women waking up this morning who have been diagnosed with breast cancer or who are trying to work through those terrible issues around making decisions with their doctor about whether to terminate a pregnancy, to wake up to this news this morning, frankly, is absolutely appalling and those comments should be condemned.”

The opposition leader, Bill Shorten, said the comments were “truly repulsive”, hurtful, ignorant and had no place in modern Australian society.

The health minister, Peter Dutton, said he believed “the medical evidence needs to be the driver in this area and it is clear that there is no link between abortion and breast cancer”.

The chief executive of the Breast Cancer Network Australia, Maxine Morand, said she worried the matter was “creating completely unnecessary anxiety about the cause of breast cancer”.

“It’s hard enough already when you get diagnosed with breast cancer: it’s a shock and it’s really emotional and it’s probably not uncommon for people to try to understand the cause and question why. The last thing you’d want is someone to create any further and unnecessary anxiety about the non-existent relationship,” she told Guardian Australia.

“This is a really common disease. Forty-two women just today have been told they have breast cancer and they’re having to deal with that diagnosis and the last thing they need is something else to cloud their experience.

“No one wants any of those women diagnosed at any point including today to be under any misconception that there’s any relationship between abortion and breast cancer.”

Morand said she was “pleased [that] Eric Abetz is now saying that he doesn’t think that there is a link” but by hosting the luncheon for Lanfranchi was “sending two very different messages”.

“On one hand he’s saying he doesn’t believe there’s a link and yet he still looks to host the person making that claim at lunch,” she said. “He’s sending confusing messages about what he believes.”
