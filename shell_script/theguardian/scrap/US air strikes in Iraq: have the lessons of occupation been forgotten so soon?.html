Title :

US air strikes in Iraq: have the lessons of occupation been forgotten so soon?

The fanatics of the Islamic State must be defeated militarily – but by Iraqis, not cynically self-interested foreign powers

• US begins air strikes against Islamic State

Contributor :

David Wearing

Public Date :

Friday 8 August 2014 15.48 BST

Content :

Yesterday, Barack Obama claimed his humanitarian conscience had moved him to intervene in northern Iraq to protect civilians from harm. It is not difficult to spot the hypocrisy or doubt the sincerity of the motives at work, especially given recent atrocities committed by Washington’s main regional ally with US-supplied arms and ammunition. Equally, given recent experience, one might question whether the US military itself can be trusted to have due regard for civilian life. But some urgent questions still need to be asked. What is going to happen to the thousands of Yazidis besieged on Mount Sinjar by the bloodthirsty fanatics of Islamic State, or to the ancient Christian communities being systematically driven out of their homes?

What is to become of the Kurdish capital, Erbil, now potentially in the crosshairs of jihadi forces? And what of Iraq, Syria and the wider region, now that Islamic State has consolidated its power and shown that it may be very far from a flash in the pan?

It is clear that humanitarian air drops are needed as a matter of urgency in northern Iraq, and these are to be welcomed. The prospect of US air strikes, however, is concerning – for two main reasons. First, as shown by Obama’s drone-led extrajudicial execution programme, there are bound to be civilian casualties. Second, as things stand, any air strikes will, de facto, be carried out on behalf of Nouri al-Maliki’s widely despised sectarian government.

There is a real danger, therefore, that air strikes could inflame the situation further, entrenching alienation among Sunni Arabs and driving more people into the arms of Islamic State. Any credible call for military action needs to show it has weighed these risks in the balance. A genuine humanitarian intervention would not be one that made the situation even more dangerous and life-threatening than it already is.

The symptoms of the crisis should be treated carefully but above all, its roots must be substantively addressed. A national unity government in Baghdad, based on the recent election results, which can credibly reach out to all communities with a new grand bargain – thus isolating Islamic State from those who may have sided with it out of desperate exasperation with Maliki – is urgently required. Time is fast running out. It beggars belief that Iraq lost a quarter of its territory two months ago and the political class in Baghdad still hasn’t got its act together. External support for the Maliki regime, not only from the US but also from Russia and especially Iran, make it less likely that the necessary political change will come.

In the end, Islamic State will have to be defeated militarily but this can only be done by Iraqis themselves, on behalf of a credible national unity government. War waged by cynically self-interested foreign forces allied to a narrowly sectarian regime will exacerbate precisely the dynamics that created this situation to begin with. That, after all, is a major lesson of the post-2003 US-led occupation.
