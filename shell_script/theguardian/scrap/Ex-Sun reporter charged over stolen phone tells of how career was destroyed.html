Title :

Ex-Sun reporter charged over stolen phone tells of how career was destroyed

Ben Ashford tells Old Bailey jury that he was sacked by the Daily Mail in the US and had his visa cancelled after his arrest

Contributor :

Lisa O'Carroll

Public Date :

Friday 8 August 2014 14.30 BST

Content :

A former Sun reporter charged with criminal offences in relation to a stolen phone has told how his career was destroyed after he was arrested.

He was sacked by the Daily Mail in the US and could not even return to collect his personal belongings after his visa was “voided”.

Ben Ashford told jurors how his dream of working in the US was shattered by his arrest in 2012 in relation to an incident that happened while he was working at the Sun three years earlier.

He been in the US working for a News Corporation outlet and after being made redundant was in the process of moving to a new job at the Mail in America when he was arrested on a brief trip back home.

“I was in the process of applying for a new job and the visa was attached to the job. After my arrest I was told not to come back into the Daily Mail office,” Ashford told jurors at the Old Bailey where he is standing trial.

“I was met by two senior members of staff. I was given a letter saying I was sacked.”

Describing his career in America he said: “It was exactly what I had wanted to do, it was fantastic. I really really enjoyed it.” He added: “It was a dream.”

Ashford has been charged with two criminal offences in relation to a stolen mobile phone he examined after it was handed into the Sun in 2009 while he was working for the paper in Manchester. He denies the charges.

The 35-year-old, who is now working for a news agency in Plymouth, is the first person on trial in relation to Operation Tuleta, Scotland Yard’s investigation into computer hacking.

He has been charged with possession of criminal property and accessing messages on the phone without authorisation of its owner, a female PR executive.

In the second day of his trial, Ashford told jurors how he had dreamed of being a journalist when he studied English at Manchester University.

He got his first break on the Croydon Advertiser after completing a postgraduate diploma in journalism and got experience on a typical local reporter’s beat, which included stories on everything from cake sales to murders.

After two years on a crime beat at a London news agency, he started getting shifts at the Sun and eventually landed a full time role in Manchester.

He said he was “gutted” by the decision to send him up north as all his police contacts and contacts with others including lobby groups and victims’ families were in London.

He eventually left to take up a new job with News Corp, the ultimate owner of the Sun, but was made redundant in the summer of 2009, the year he got offered the job on the Mail in New York.

He told jurors that because he lost his job in the US, his visa application was “voided” and after consulting two emigration lawyers was told that he could not return to America.

“I wasn’t able to even go back to collect my belongings,” Ashford said.

The jury has heard how three years earlier in 2009, Ashford was despatched to meet a tipster who had contacted the Sun offices in London claiming to have evidence of a celebrity cheating on his long-term girlfriend.

He met the tipster who handed him a phone which three days later he handed back to the owner, a PR executive.

The jury heard that before handing it back, Ashford had examined the contents of the phone and produced an 18 page dossier for his London bosses containing details of “saucy messages” and “X-rated photos” exchanged between the owner and a TV personality who cannot be named.

He told jurors he found it “difficult” to recall events of three years ago because he was so busy at the time, he hadn’t stopped to consider what he was doing.

Asked by his counsel, Orlando Pownall, if had spent much time considering events between 11 November 2009 when he gave a statement to the police about the phone and 2012 when he was arrested, he replied: “Absolutely none whatsoever.”

Asked how much time he had spent thinking about these events since his arrest, he replied: “Practically every waking moment I’ve thought about it.”

The trial continues.
