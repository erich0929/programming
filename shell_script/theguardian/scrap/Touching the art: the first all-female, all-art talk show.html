Title :

Touching the art: the first all-female, all-art talk show

Comedian Casey Jane Ellison becomes an art-world cliche – bored, nonchalant and dressed all in black – then asks industry insiders awkward questions. The results are a riot

Contributor :

Dale Berning

Public Date :

Friday 8 August 2014 16.02 BST

Content :

"Is art evil? Is the internet dead? Why is everything so po-mo?" Standup comic Casey Jane Ellison would like to know. She's pulling off a first with Touching the Art, an all-female, all-art comedy talk show. She set the tone from the off, taking on a deadpan-bitchy persona and joking that the show is "where we discuss the art world's least favourite subject: art". The show premiered on Ovation TV in July, but two episodes in, it's already clear that there's nothing else like it out there.

The format is simple: with a trio of respected art-world guests, from New York Times critic Jori Finkel to photographer Catherine Opie and founder of ForYourArt.com Bettina Korek, Ellison leads a debate on three topics, such as art and gender, or the art market. Cumbersome questions pile up: why can't women artists sell their work properly? Is art better if the person making it is a celeb? A closing Lightning Round makes the panel answer a last and largely unanswerable question – What is art? or What is art worth? – in three words or less. "You're all wrong," is Ellison's invariable conclusion, riffing on the idea of the critic as know-it-all.

What makes the show both painful and totally compelling is that it almost doesn't know what genre it is: this could be parody, performance art, standup, serious critique, or all of them at once. The disconnect between Ellison's head-shaking, finger-wagging valley-girl persona and the panel's candidness is disconcerting. "Don't you think it's so funny how women artists can't sell their art?" she asks in episode one, then flicks her hair in Kardashian-like exasperation – "No, I know. Yes." – when Korek plainly doesn't find it amusing. 

Ellison embodies every art-world-insider stereotype, feigning arrogance, boredom, nostril-flaring irritation and irony, while clad in all-black, down to her lipstick. But the questions are real, and the guests answer them sincerely. Their willingness to open up came as a real shock to Ellison: "These conversations happen constantly in private, but historically the art world has been less than eager to have them out in the open." This is why Touching the Art works: with the art world at times as opaque as the murky world of real estate, perhaps uncomfortable comedy is the only way in.

To write the show, Ellison thinks of questions she's always wanted the answers to. The discussions so far have been awkward "because we pulled back the curtain a little", she explains. "Also, all the guests hate me – so that was awkward, too." An emerging artist as well as a comedian, she brilliantly captures how stifling a world it can be, though both she and the show's creator, Shaw Bowman, emphasise that, above all, it aims to make the art world accessible, in a fun way. "One of the main goals was to reach not only people in the art world, but also people who might not care about art or even 'hate' it," says Ellison. "Honestly, many people in the art world 'hate' art, too."

The show plays on that ambivalence. "We wanted to make Casey the butt of the joke," says Bowman, "not laugh at the art world. And everyone is clearly enjoying themselves. Casey is the obstinate character giving voice to anxieties around art."

Obstinate she is, and it leads to some brilliant moments, like her constant passive-aggressive mentions of the fact that she feels no need to justify the show's gender bias: "We have an all-female panel joining us today." She squares up to the camera: "Is that an issue? Because we only have women on this show, and we don't really feel the need to discuss or defend that."

The first two episodes were a pilot double bill, and the team is now regrouping to write the next instalments. Ellison wants to get out and about to cover big events – Art Basel, the Armory Show – beyond the studio. So watch this space: there's much more awkwardness to come.
