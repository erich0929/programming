Title :

Olympic afterlife: the real legacy of the London Games for Stratford

With construction limiting public services and investors cashing in on ‘the Olympic effect’, Stratford’s residents are wondering whether hosting the Games was really worth it

Contributor :

Public Date :

Friday 8 August 2014 10.46 BST

Content :

We moved to Stratford in the autumn of 2011 – an exigency forced on us by London’s housing bubble. It was a reluctant emigration: the river Lea marked the eastern boundary of my experience of the capital, barring a few brief but harrowing visits after falling asleep on night buses as a student 10 years earlier. There was only one good pub, and the 2012 Olympics, about which I felt no joy, loomed large over the area.

My block of flats was in a converted 1970s office building on the traffic island at the heart of Stratford, next to the old shopping centre. The latter offered the same pound shops and sportswear emporiums you can find on every British high street, but it also housed a thriving market, with a fish stall next to a Polish deli and an artisanal cheesemaker. It was 21st-century East End commerce in microcosm, peacefully cohabiting for once, rather than engaged in a fight to the death.

Because it is a thoroughfare, the centre was (and still is) left open all night, which is when things got really interesting. After the sausages and whelks had been cleared away, rollerbladers with boomboxes would pirouette across the terrazzo, dive-bombing drunks as they meandered home from the station. To my surprise, I found the place appealing to my romantic streak – I was on JG Ballard’s concrete island, a not unhappy castaway.

Like the rest of Stratford, though, the old shopping centre was at the mercy of the gods of Olympus – and it clearly didn’t fit with the image Newham council wanted to project to the world. As the Games approached, the council tried to hide the building behind a particularly egregious piece of public art: a shoal of iridescent lozenges on sticks. These concealed nothing, merely added a citrusy top-note to the dog’s dinner of the ring road.

A more substantial threat came from Westfield, the gigantic mall and “gateway to the Games”, which had opened shortly before I moved to the area. I was sceptical about its boring shops and the threat they posed to local businesses. Above all, I was repulsed by the cacophonous architecture of the place, and the fake public space inside. But eventually I learned to love Westfield, too, for the enormous relief of no longer having to go to Oxford street for shopping – and for the people-watching opportunities it afforded. Thankfully, it also turned out to do the old shopping centre little harm – footfall has if anything increased there, thanks to Stratford’s transformation into a shopping “destination”.

If, however, I was charmed by Stratford and seduced by Westfield, there was one big unavoidable blot on my new home’s escutcheon: the Olympics. It wasn’t that I was outraged by the abuse of the existing landscape. The psycho-geographer’s romance of the Lea valley was never high on my agenda – I like post-industrial wastelands as much as the next man, but to leave a huge tranche of inner-city land unoccupied in the middle of a housing shortage is stupid. My problem was more with what would be built on it, and for whom.

The press image showing Danny Boyle towering over a model set for his opening ceremony didn’t assuage my suspicions that the pageant would be a celebration of all that is cloying, limp, and cap-doffing about our sceptered isles. The architecture of the proposed venues seemed either anodyne or meretricious, and the idea of “legacy”, after the disasters of Athens and Beijing, an outright lie.

Another irritant was that the Olympics had, contrary to the nebulous promise of “inspiring a generation”, actually made swimming and cycling much harder to do in Stratford. The local pool had been closed because asbestos was discovered during refurbishment earlier that year, and the council had clearly not bothered to sort it out because Zaha Hadid was coming with her voluptuous aquatic centre. And the relatively car-free cycle route into town, which began on the linear park called the Greenway that straddles Bazalgette’s monstrous east-west sewage pipe, was quickly closed for “security reasons” (it went through the Olympic park). Instead, you had to cycle down the thunderous high street and over Bow roundabout, the nemesis of many a cyclist, before being spat out onto the thin blue line of one of London’s most dangerous cycle superhighways. This brush with death was a nerve-jangling way to start the day, more effective than any caffeine jolt.

As the games approached, the religious maniacs who clamoured outside the station grew ever more hysterical. Others were getting a bit carried away, too: a friend had volunteered to take part in the opening ceremony, and her enthusiasm – which regularly took her to a gigantic tent in an industrial estate in Dagenham for rehearsals – puzzled me. I couldn’t imagine the ceremony being anything other than rubbish, but her assurances that I was wrong gradually chipped away at my scepticism, and when the day finally came, I was feeling more open-minded. Crucially, I was also drunk.

Watching the spectacle on a big screen in nearby Haggerston park surrounded by hundreds of other inebriates, I was won over. I even attended a few events, including the Paralympic session when George Osborne turned up to award a medal and was resoundingly booed. The look on his face was priceless; it had all been worth it, almost.

Admittedly, living in Stratford during the games was at times both tedious and alarming. The station became an impenetrable knot of visitors and paramilitary police, and Westfield was clogged by a fatberg of witless tourists. Meanwhile University College London was trying to evict an entire housing estate to build an eastern campus on its ruins – a scheme that thankfully came to nothing, after a spirited campaign by the residents – and Anish Kapoor’s red steel prolapse reared on the skyline. It was only a small consolation that we were regularly treated to the sight of off-duty athletes roaming the streets, like nylon-clad gazelles escaped from a game reserve. They even crossed the ring road to the old part of town: thrillingly, I once spotted Louis Smith visiting the Caribbean restaurant on the ground floor of our apartment block.

But the athletes didn’t hang around, and we were left with something less aesthetically pleasing altogether. The Olympic park, now renamed after her majesty, turned out to be a representation of her kingdom in miniature: a post-apocalyptic Disneyland where Mad Max meets the Teletubbies, with wobbly green protuberances undulating among acres of tarmac. Perhaps it’s unfair to pass judgment at this stage; the place is still a building site, and will be for years to come.

The stadium is currently fenced off – it’s being circumcised to prepare it for occupation by West Ham United FC – and Boris has recently proclaimed the establishment of an “Olympicopolis” next to the aquatic centre, with hopes of enticing outposts of the V&A museum, UCL and other cultural institutions. The media centre is being turned into – hold on to your lunch – “Here East: a world-leading creative and digital cluster and London’s home for making.” Despite feeling provisional and patchy, on sunny days the park is full of young families all clearly having a whale of a time. But then, faced with the opportunity of a picnic in a litter strewn lay-by, the Dunkirk spirit of the British has always been irrepressible.

Meanwhile, the fringes of the park are increasingly hemmed-in by huge and hideous developer-designed towers cashing in on the Olympic effect. The ones on the high street, with names like “Halo” and “Stratosphere”, are garlanded with balconies looking onto the smog-filled canyon outside. I’ve never seen anyone sitting out on these balconies – why would you, it’s a horrible view – but they weren’t designed to appeal to their residents, rather to overseas investors. Ikea’s property arm is building a huge mixed-use development south of the high street, and a red-sheathed ziggurat has been put up next to Westfield, full of overseas students being battery-farmed for rent.

Inside the park, the former athletes’ village – which was sold to the Qataris at a knock-down price after the games – is now hilariously called East Village. It’s a sterile grid of apartment blocks surrounding gated courtyards. Some of these buildings are clad with cast panels representing scenes from the Elgin marbles (Mussolini might have approved, but wouldn’t something from Olympia have been more appropriate?).

People are beginning to move in to the area, and I’m sure it’s a nice enough place, if you live in abject terror of all the things that make cities worthwhile. But whether all these new homes will benefit existing residents is another question: while house prices in Stratford seem relatively unaffected by the games, especially when compared to neighbouring Hackney, the amount of affordable housing – and its degree of affordability – seems unlikely to do much to alleviate the borough’s housing shortage.

Some bits of Stratford did change for the better during the Games. On the fringe of the park, forming a not entirely successful bridge to the impoverished streets of Leyton, is Chobham Academy, a grand new school which cost £33m. Its architecture might not be to all tastes, or indeed to many, but its generosity is not to be sniffed at in these straitened times, and the space-age atrium makes some fancy hotels look distinctly shabby in comparison. Meanwhile, a vacant lot on the square by my apartment was filled with a small campus shared by Birkbeck and the University of East London. Designed by the architectural firm Make, it’s nothing special to look at either but it does bring an educational presence into the centre of town: a welcome move, not least because the local library had been shut for refurbishment shortly before I moved in.

Outside the library a boulder commemorates poet Gerard Manley Hopkins, who was improbably enough a local boy. It had originally been embellished with some lines from his Wreck of the Deutschland: “Loathed for a love men knew in them/ banned by the land of their birth/ Rhine refused them/ Thames would ruin them …’ This sentiment felt particularly poignant in Newham, where ethnic minorities are in the majority; even more poignantly, the plaque had been prised off for scrap metal leaving the monument just a mute stone outside the shuttered library. It didn’t seem like a good sign.

When the building finally reopened, I went in for a look around. The collection of books was depressingly scanty, as they usually are in libraries these days, but the space was not unpleasant and clearly popular. Even better was the conversation I overheard between two teenagers studying there. One was explaining to his friend: “The church is just bullshit fam, it’s all about property and money.” The library was still doing its job – and the Hopkins poem had been restored to its rock.

I would have liked to have stayed on in Stratford. I wanted the aquatic centre to be my local pool: after putting up with all the faff and fanfare of the games, that only seemed fair. But London’s rental market discourages settling down, and I moved out of the area in the autumn of 2012. I’ve been back a few times since, including a trip to the aquatic centre shortly after it opened to the public. When you get over the confusing anticlimax of having to enter via a small side door, and when you get used to the spooky emptiness of the place, the building is, unquestionably, an impressive place to swim. Lying on your back, drifting beneath the biomorphic bulge of the wooden-slatted ceiling, is perhaps what being keel-hauled feels like. After going through the Olympics together, Stratford and I know that feeling well.

Tom Wilkinson is history editor of the Architectural Review. His new book Bricks and Mortals: Ten Great Buildings and the People they Made is published by Bloomsbury
